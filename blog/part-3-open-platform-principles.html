Principles of an Open Agentic Workforce Platform

The decisions enterprises make about their agentic workforce platform now will echo for years to come.

Let's explore the principles that separate open, flexible platforms from proprietary ones that create dependency.

The Vendor Lock-In Trap

The Risk with Agent Platforms

Today, some platforms offer a seductive promise: "Use our agent framework, our infrastructure, our services, we'll handle everything."

The hidden cost? You're locked into:

One vendor's agent framework (can't use emerging alternatives)

Their pricing model (subject to their price increases)

Their roadmap (features arrive on their timeline)

Their regional availability (limited by their data centers)

Their compliance certifications (hope they support your regulations)

When the best agent framework changes (and I think we're still for from a stable endpoint), what do you do now?

Principle 1: Bring Your Own Agent (BYOA)

The Foundation of Flexibility

Bring Your Own Agent means the platform supports any agent framework without forcing standardization.

Why This Matters:

The enterprise cannot afford framework lock-in. Teams will choose different agents based on:

  • Task requirements (some frameworks excel at reasoning, others at code)
  • Existing investments (teams already using specific frameworks)
  • Emerging capabilities (new frameworks appear constantly)
  • Vendor preferences (avoiding single-vendor dependency)

A platform that demands "use only our agent framework" creates the same lock-in enterprises spent decades escaping from proprietary solutions.

You need a platform that ensures consistency while teams maintain choice and autonomy.

This of course does NOT prevent your from using hosted proprietary models right now. We understand, they might be the best fit for some of your workloads right now. But at least make sure you give yourself the opportunity to consider other or a mixture of options in the future.

Principle 2: Open Source Foundations

Why Open Source Matters

The enterprise platform market has established a clear pattern: open source foundations enable vendor competition while avoiding lock-in.

Open Platforms

Characteristics of Open Platforms:

  • Core orchestration, APIs, and standards are open
  • Multiple vendors contribute in providing solutions
  • Customers can swap out components without losing their agentic workforce
  • Community contributions drive innovation faster than any single vendor

This enables transparency, vendor competition, community innovation and trust in the platform.

Principle 3: Ecosystem Diversity

No Single Vendor Has Everything

Enterprises need more than one vendor can provide:

LLM Providers: OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, open models, regional providers
Agent Frameworks: Claude Code, LangChain, Google ADK, CrewAI, AutoGen, proprietary
Observability: Prometheus, Datadog, Dynatrace, Splunk
Storage: PostgreSQL, MongoDB, vector databases, object storage
Business Systems: Salesforce, SAP, ServiceNow, custom applications

The Power of Integration

Instead of replacing this ecosystem, the platform must integrate with it:

Open APIs: Well-documented, version-stable interfaces that partners can implement

Standard Formats: Skills, configurations, and integrations work across vendors

Partner Participation: Vendors bring their own unique capabilities to the platform

Building value on top: Any company can build value on top of the platform

Customer Choice: Pick best-of-breed components for each need

This is vendor-neutral infrastructure: the platform orchestrates while customers choose capabilities.

The Eight Core Principles

Combined with the challenges from the previous blog, I believe that brings us 8 valuable principles:

  1. Bring Your Own Agent: Support heterogeneous frameworks, don't force standardization
  2. Open Source Foundation: Open APIs and standards prevent vendor lock-in
  3. Ecosystem Diversity: Integrate with partners, don't replace them
  4. Unified Visibility: See what all agents are doing across frameworks and locations
  5. Consistent Governance: Same policies whether agents run locally or in the cloud
  6. Shared services: Agents become more lean and can rely on capabilities provided by the platform
  7. Security by Default: Sandboxing, access control, and audit built in
  8. Hybrid Deployment: Run agents anywhere: on-premises, cloud, edge
